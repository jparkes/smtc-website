Photos numbers of a brown color refer to what is at least partially an African-American section and is a separate area of the same cemetery or separate cemetery across a deep gulley divide to the north 75 yards, but still fronting the Big Dry Creek Road. Some of these graves may have been listed among the Salem (black) Cemetery of the book Giles County Cemeteries page 475,others were ommitted. Below is a listing of the 10 names listed. There was no other information:

DOUGLAS, Mary/Hudley  -- LAIRD, Alice/Tom -- MASON, Jim/Mariah -- MAYFIELD, Jenny -- REYNOLDS, Billy/Fannie/Tom.

The other irony in this sprawling grave space which is divided across a deep gorge is that many of the graves with marked stones, even known African-Americans such as William Bailey were included in with th common listing in previous efforts to capture the grave history. Truth be known these two sections may have sprang up because of religious divisions, cultural division, or even neighborhood divisions. The north section is very sprawling and not in orderly rows meaning it was never surveyed, typical of old black cemeteries and some white cemeteries. I have a great-great uncle, a single white man, by the name of Richard Warden (a businessman) that owned a farm across the road, but 1/4 mile south from this place. He was of the Christian Religion and it is said he was buried on his farm in what is now a lost grave. So unorganized lost graves are not of one class or culture.
We are less than privy to the business of determining skin tone by looking at the grave site. If that is necessary for you in understanding your heritage you can get a better feel by researching the census records. I have presented the information as I found it. This is more precise (but non segregated) than previous grave historians. If you can shed light on this subject please write me at:WayneAL1@AOL.COM and we will share your thoughts with the public.

Both of these cemeteries were covered in brush, growing freely and covering the stones, making it hard to find the stones. Fall leaves also may have covered some of the stones that had fallen. That is partially the reason why you see the above note - stones not found. Several of the stones in the south section of the cemetery are inscribed with the message: Member of the Salem Methodist Church, which was evidently the founding reason for the white section & maybe also the black area of this cemetery.
