First Man Legally Hung in Maury County 

By Rick Gray

22 Jun 2005 

The date of 2 April 1827 holds an important place in the history of Maury County. On this date, James R. Bennett became the first man legally hung in Maury County. Although this was Maury County�s first legal hanging, the practice was an ordinary part of the day. The hanging of James R. Bennett would have lasting effects on the citizens of Maury County and the family of James R. Bennett.

 Almost 67 years after the event, Caroline O�Reilly Nicholson, widow of A. O. P. Nicholson, would recall, �I was haunted by the uncanny face of the doomed man.� Mrs. Nicholson did not attend the hanging herself. She wrote that she was one of a number of girls who went around to Mrs. Voorhies to get a look at Bennett. They saw him come out of the jail and get into a cart that would carry him to the gallows. She wrote that the gallows stood at the bottom north of the old cemetery, and that they were left there long after the hanging as a warning to evil doers.

 The hanging affected Thomas D. Cathey in such a way that he would tell members of this family that he never wanted to see another man hanged.

 The effects on the Bennett family were long lasting as well. Murder seemed to follow the family in the coming years. Ezekiel Bennett, son of James, would be murdered by an in-law about 50 years after his father was hanged. James� grandson, Israel Thomason, would later be arrested for the murder of another.

 Throughout the years, many questions have shrouded this important event in the history of Maury County. Along the way, many of the facts became hidden or became so embellished with each retelling of the story that fact and fiction were hard to separate. In most of the retellings, the victim of James R. Bennett�s crime was left unnamed. It was told that he was a soldier, which led Marise Lightfoot to research the crime and hanging. She found many facts in her research, but could never find the victims name. Everything was there about the crime and the hanging, but the victims name could not be found in the Maury County records. That question and many others were finally answered when Vera Niemiec, of Lincoln Park, Michigan, discovered that James R. Bennett was her great-great-grandfather and wanted to know more about the events surrounding the crime that led to her Bennett�s hanging. Through her research much of the fact and fiction were separated.

 Her search for the truth led her to Nashville where she unearthed the court files that had been sent from Columbia to the Supreme Court in Nashville. These files opened the doors of her research into the case.

 She learned that the actual crime took place on 17 February 1826, when Bennett shot Thomas Callahan. Callahan lingered until the 18th and died. The bullet struck Callahan near the eleventh rib, and by the time the doctor arrived, there was no hope for his life. Bennett used a rifle to kill Callahan. The court records revealed the value of the rifle to be worth five to fifteen dollars.

 This was not Maury County�s first murder or murder trial, but this one would have a different outcome. The first murder trial in Maury County took place in 1816 when William Magill was tried for the murder of Dr. Charles Simpson. During the course of the trial, Magill broke jail and escaped. The first person accused of murder in Maury County was Nancy Christian, accused of murdering her child, but these charges were dropped in 1817. In October 1825, Joachim Lindsey murdered his wife, but made his escape. A few months before Bennett shot Callahan, two men were gunned down on the public square in Columbia. Once again, the murderers made their escape. Bennett would not find his charges dropped or make his escape. He would face trial and the outcome would mean the end of his life.

 The jury was summoned and Bennett faced trial for the murder of Thomas Callahan. The jury was made up of Samuel H. Williams, William Allen, John O. Davidson, John Farney, Ephraim E. Davidson, William Kerr, William Pillow, Anthony M. Copeland, William Voorhies, Thomas Gill, Franklin R. Houston, and William Jennings. With each witness brought before the court, more and more details of the life and character of Bennett were revealed.  Much of the testimony centered on Bennett�s drinking habits, and his 1822 conviction of counterfeiting which resulted in his property being sold at public auction. Witnesses stated his mind became �disordered at this.� One witness recalled that Bennett was drunk during the sale, and �talked of killing the officers.�

 Besides the testimony about Bennett�s character, one can learn a few interesting things about the Blowing Springs area from testimony given during the trial. Witnesses alluded to the fact that there was a gang of outlaws who roamed the hills south of the Blowing Springs, and that Indians were still traveling along the Military Road.

 Some of the testimony of witnesses revealed some interesting facts about the jails of the time. Two jailors testified in the trial. One remembered that while in jail Bennett was �frequently going round the jail all night flashing pistols.� The other jailor revealed that Bennett was drunk when put in jail, then sobered up in a day, then got hold of more whiskey and was drunk again. We think the jails are liberal today, but back then a prisoner still could access his guns and his whiskey.

 One of the witnesses during the trial was Chiles McGee. It was not revealed during the course of the trial that McGee was prejudiced against Bennett. McGee had been one of the prosecutors during the trial for counterfeiting which resulted in Bennett�s conviction of those charges in 1822. McGee revealed that Bennett was a �judicious man� and �drank too much as well as himself.�

 The judge did try to help Bennett out a little. He urged the jury not to pay too much attention to the drinking, as every man saw a drunk in a different way.

 Perhaps the most damaging testimony came from Archibald James and Samuel McConnell.

 Archibald James traveled 81 miles to testify at the trial. He recalled during the month of February 1826, he was traveling on the road passing Bennett�s house. About a quarter mile before he got to Bennett�s, he heard the report of a gun. As he approached Bennett�s house, Bennett came out to the road with blood on his hands, proclaiming, �I have caught the villain at last.�

 Bennett invited James, along with a Mr. Smith who was traveling with some wagons ahead of James, to come into the house.

 James went into the house to find a man lying on the floor bleeding. Bennett revealed to him that he had shot the man, and if James did not believe him to ask his wife.

 James described the injured man as �an old infirm man, lying groaning, and I heard him ask that a doctor might be sent for.�

 James left the Bennett house, traveled down the road about a half mile and camped for the night.

 James met Samuel McConnell and told him what had happened at Bennett�s house. The two men went back to Bennett�s house where James testified that Bennett seemed to be in something of a rage and �looked strange but was not drunk.�

 Cross-examination of James further clarified that James did not think that Bennett was drunk, and stressed that James had never seen Bennett before this meeting.

 McConnell was the next witness called. He recalled seeing Callahan going to Bennett�s the night before the shooting. He described Callahan as �old and infirm and badly dressed.� The next morning McConnell went to Bennett�s to borrow an auger. When he arrived at Bennett�s still house, he saw Bennett and Callahan sitting together, drinking and repeating verses. He said, �They appeared to be friendly to each other.� He did not return until the next day, when he returned with Archibald James. He revealed the time to be between sunset and dark. When he went into the home, he found Callahan �lying down, groaning and bleeding.�

 In the course of his testimony, McConnell revealed things told to him by Callahan before he died. McConnell testified that Callahan told him that Bennett was a murderer and had wounded him without cause or reason. Bennett�s response at hearing this was that he thought he was master of his own house, that he had �aimed to shoot him through the heart�, and he did not think he had made too bad of an aim.

 Callahan also revealed to McConnell that he was inhabiting a camp near Bennett�s house, and that Bennett said, �he had made too free in his house.� Bennett described Callahan as a pilfering old villain and that it was his �right to kill such.�

 The last thing Callahan revealed to McConnell was that he had been in the artillery for 21 years and that he was on his way to meet his son.

 With each bit of testimony, Bennett�s fate was sealed. Despite the urging of the judge not to pay too much attention to the drinking, Bennett was found guilty and sentenced to hang on 26 September 1826. The jury�s statement being �on that day he shall be conveyed by the sheriff of Maury County to a gallows erected on the common of Columbia and then hung by the neck until dead, and may the Lord have mercy on his soul.�

 The date of 26 September 1826 went unchallenged for many years, until Mrs. Niemiec discovered that Bennett appeared in court again in January 1827. He appeared in person before the Supreme Court and made an appeal. The appeal was denied and a notation in the Supreme Court records revealed that the hanging took place on 2 April 1827.

 The hanging was a big event, drawing huge crowds to Columbia. The site chosen for the hanging was a small bottom on the Duck River. This site is about where the John Harlan Willis Bridge is today on Highway 31. It was said that even whole families attended the hanging together.

 The sheriff, Nimrod Porter, was in charge of the hanging. After securing the noose, Bennett was moved into position over the trapdoor. The records do not reveal to us if Bennett was blindfolded or if his head was covered.

 Porter went down underneath the gallows, took an axe and knocked the props from under the trapdoor. Bennett then dropped through the floor. Porter was paid $12.50 for building the gallows and his services at the hanging.

 Bennett�s body was taken to Blowing Springs Cemetery for burial. His grave lay unmarked for many years. He was survived by his wife, the former Rachel Alexander, and four children: Mary (about 7 years old), Israel W. (about 6 years old), James K. (about 4 years old), and Ezekiel (about 2 years old).

 Rachel Bennett married John Davis on 23 Feb 1828 in Maury County and had six more children: Lucinda (married James Brock), Alzada (married James Chapman), William, Allen (married Minerva ?), Jane and E. D. Davis. Some have speculated that she and John are buried at Blowing Springs. She died after 1860.

 Mary Bennett married William C. Thomason, son of William Thomason and Polly Morand, on 4 Mar 1839 in Maury County. Together they were blessed with 12 children: Angeline Thomason (wife of John McClain); Israel Washington Thomason (married Lillian King & Ann Elizabeth Chumbley); John F. Thomason (married Mary ?); Joseph Alexander Thomason (married Elizabeth Chumbley and Lucretia Munford); Caledonia Thomason (married Eli King); Margaret Thomason; Nancy Thomason; Rachel Thomason; Lizzie Thomason; Kate Thomason (married Samuel Washington Hedge & Seaton Higginbotham); Isabel Thomason (married Samuel Washington Hedge); Jackson C. Thomason (married Nancy Jane Blackburn & Bertie Mae Doyle); and J. Monroe Thomason. Mary died after 1870.

 Israel W. Bennett married Malinda N. Allison on 16 Jan 1849 in Lewis County. The family can later be found in both Lewis County and Lawrence County on census records.

 James K. Bennett married Sidney Jane Devasier on 30 Nov 1840 in Lawrence County. He and Sidney were the parents of six children: William F. Bennett (married Martha Rogers), Aaron Alexander Bennett, Susan Jane Bennett, Druscilla Ellen Bennett (married Green Drewry Devasier), James K. Bennett (married twice, Mary A. & Alice), and Jesse Monroe N. Bennett. James died 28 Oct 1900 in Lewis County, Tennessee.

 Ezekiel Bennett married Mary ? and can be found on some census records as a farmer in Lawrence County. He was killed by an in-law about 50 years after his father�s death.
